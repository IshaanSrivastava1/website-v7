LED vs. Fluorescent Lighting

A Life Cycle Consultant's Report for Bombardier

MIE315: Design for the Environment · University of Toronto · April 2023

Life Cycle Assessment · OpenLCA · TRACI 2.1 · GWP · Financial Analysis · Sensitivity Analysis

What We Built

A consultant's report evaluating whether Bombardier should replace the fluorescent lighting at its Saint-Laurent manufacturing facility (a 1,200,000 sq. ft. site with 4,367 overhead fixtures) with LED (Light Emitting Diode) alternatives. Working in a four-person team for MIE315: Design for the Environment at the University of Toronto, we treated Bombardier as a real client and delivered a recommendation backed by four analyses: financial, environmental, societal, and uncertainty. I led the Financial Analysis and the Process-Based LCA (Life Cycle Assessment), the two quantitative pillars behind the final verdict: switch to LEDs. Completed April 2023.

Thesis

A lighting choice that looks expensive up front can be the smarter decision once you account for its full life cycle. By comparing LEDs against LFLs (Linear Fluorescent Lights) across cost, carbon, and social impact rather than sticker price alone, the report makes the case that LEDs are the better investment for Bombardier on every axis that matters.

Design Decisions

  • Scoped the analysis to the use and end-of-life stages. Rather than model every life cycle stage, we framed the system boundary from the perspective of a corporate consumer who cares most about their own spending, which begins once bulbs are received. This kept the analysis focused on the stages Bombardier could actually act on.
  • Chose lumens per kWh (kilowatt-hour) as the functional unit. Comparing bulbs by energy efficiency, rather than raw brightness or wattage, gave a like-for-like basis for comparison that ties directly to both operating cost and environmental impact.
  • Selected GWP (Global Warming Potential) as the environmental impact category. From the many categories available in the LCA, we prioritized GWP because it is the most widely understood measure of environmental harm, making the finding legible to a non-technical client.
  • Ran sensitivity analyses on the most uncertain inputs. Because the LCA relied on secondary data, the team stress-tested the three least certain parameters (manufacturing electricity, transportation, and warehouse electricity) at plus or minus 25% and 50% to confirm the conclusion held even if the data were off.

My Work: Financial Analysis

I built the cost case comparing the two alternatives over their full life cycles rather than on purchase price alone.

  • Set a 15-year timeframe. LEDs and LFLs last for different periods (7.5 years vs. 3 years under the client's usage), so I set the comparison window to their lowest common multiple. This ensured neither alternative gained an unfair edge from where its replacement cycles happened to land.
  • Applied the equivalent annual worth method. Since lighting is an ongoing operating tool rather than a one-time purchase with a fixed end date, I expressed each option as an equivalent annual cost. This was more meaningful to a client than a single lump-sum figure and made the yearly savings directly visible.
  • Modelled the full cost picture. I accounted for capital and installation costs, operational energy draw, periodic replacements from premature failures, and end-of-life replacement, using the client's real facility data (4,367 fixtures, 18 hours/day usage) and Bombardier's own return on invested capital of 2.24% as the minimum acceptable rate of return.

The result: despite a $100,441 CAD upfront cost for LEDs versus $0 to keep the existing fluorescents, the switch produces equivalent annual savings of $35,319.79 CAD per year, driven by lower energy use, fewer premature failures, and a longer replacement cycle.

My Work: Process-Based Life Cycle Analysis

I quantified the environmental case using OpenLCA software and the TRACI 2.1 impact method.

  • Built the life cycle inventory for both alternatives. I compiled the materials, energy, and transport inputs for each bulb across the use and end-of-life stages, drawing on secondary data where primary data was not feasible to obtain.
  • Focused the impact analysis on GWP. Measured in kg CO2e, this gave a single, widely understood indicator to compare the two options and isolate their biggest emission contributors.

The result: fluorescent bulbs carry a 97.69% greater GWP than LEDs, a gap driven largely by their higher energy demand and the emissions embedded in their materials and disposal.

Outcomes

As an academic exercise, the report's recommendation was unambiguous: Bombardier should switch to LEDs, a verdict supported by all four analyses.

The two headline numbers both came from my work: equivalent annual savings of $35,319.79 CAD per year on the financial side, and a 97.69% greater GWP for fluorescents on the environmental side. The societal analysis reinforced the case, since fluorescent manufacturing exposes workers and consumers to mercury, phosphor dust, and UV radiation that LEDs avoid, and the uncertainty analysis confirmed the environmental finding held across every sensitivity scenario.

Limitations worth noting: the study relied on secondary data, focused only on GWP rather than the full range of environmental categories, and omitted packaging data. The team flagged these as areas for a more comprehensive follow-up study.
